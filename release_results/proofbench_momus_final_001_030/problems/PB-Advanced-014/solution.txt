### **Part 2: The Final Synthesis**

**The Architect's Log (Hemingway Style):**
The problem was a matter of parity and residues. Odd numbers are trapped in a cycle of four; even numbers have more room to move. We found that equality is only possible if they share a parity, and if odd, a residue modulo four. To prove this was enough, we built a three-stage engine. First, we let the larger number grow until it was far ahead. We proved this growth was inevitable. Second, we aligned their residues using the parity of the steps. Finally, we used a linear sequence to bring them to a ratio of one to three, where a single final operation made them one. The math is solid. The path is clear.

**The Final Blueprint:**

**The Problem**
Two distinct positive integers $a$ and $b$ are written on a blackboard. At each step, one must apply either $x \mapsto x+2$ or $x \mapsto 3x$ to each number. Find all pairs $(a, b)$ such that the two numbers can be made equal in a finite number of steps.

**I. Necessary Conditions**
Let $a_n$ and $b_n$ be the values of the two numbers after $n$ steps.
1.  **Parity:** Both operations $x \mapsto x+2$ and $x \mapsto 3x$ preserve the parity of $x$ (since $3x \equiv 1 \cdot x \pmod 2$). Thus, $a_n \equiv a \pmod 2$ and $b_n \equiv b \pmod 2$. If $a_n = b_n$, then $a \equiv b \pmod 2$.
2.  **Modulo 4 (Odd Case):** Assume $a$ and $b$ are odd. If $x$ is odd, $x \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ or $x \equiv 3 \pmod 4$.
    *   If $x \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, then $x+2 \equiv 3 \pmod 4$ and $3x \equiv 3 \pmod 4$.
    *   If $x \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, then $x+2 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ and $3x \equiv 9 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$.
    In all cases, for any odd $x$, the result of either operation is $x' \equiv x+2 \pmod 4$.
    After $n$ steps, $a_n \equiv a + 2n \pmod 4$ and $b_n \equiv b + 2n \pmod 4$.
    If $a_n = b_n$, then $a + 2n \equiv b + 2n \pmod 4$, which implies $a \equiv b \pmod 4$.
3.  **Necessity Summary:** Equality is reachable only if $a$ and $b$ have the same parity, and if they are odd, $a \equiv b \pmod 4$.

**II. Sufficiency of the Conditions**
Let $a$ and $b$ be distinct positive integers satisfying the conditions in Section I. Assume $a < b$ without loss of generality.

**Stage A: Expansion and Residue Alignment**
Apply $Op_a: x \mapsto x+2$ and $Op_b: x \mapsto 3x$ for $n$ steps.
The state becomes $(a', b') = (a + 2n, 3^n b)$. Let $d' = b' - a'$.
1.  **Existence of Magnitude:** We require $d' \ge 2a' - 2$, which is $3^n b - (a + 2n) \ge 2(a + 2n) - 2$.
    Rearranging gives $3^n b - 6n \ge 3a - 2$.
    Let $f(n) = 3^n b - 6n$.
    $f(n+1) - f(n) = (3^{n+1} b - 6(n+1)) - (3^n b - 6n) = 2 \cdot 3^n b - 6$.
    Since $b \ge 1$, for $n \ge 1$, $2 \cdot 3^n b - 6 \ge 2(3) - 6 = 0$.
    For $n \ge 2$, $2 \cdot 3^n b - 6 \ge 2(9) - 6 = 12$.
    Thus, $f(n)$ is strictly increasing for $n \ge 2$. Since $3^n$ grows exponentially and $6n$ grows linearly, $\lim_{n \to \infty} f(n) = \infty$.
    There exists a constant $N$ such that for all $n \ge N$, $3^n b - 6n \ge 3a - 2$.

2.  **Residue Alignment:**
    We examine $d' = 3^n b - a - 2n \pmod 4$. Note $3^n \equiv (-1)^n \pmod 4$.
    *   **Case: $a, b$ are even.** We require $d' \equiv 2 \pmod 4$.
        $d' \equiv (-1)^n b - a - 2n \pmod 4$. Let $r_a, r_b \in \{0, 2\}$ be the residues of $a, b \pmod 4$.
        *   If $(r_a, r_b) = (0, 0)$: $d' \equiv -2n$. Pick $n$ odd $\implies d' \equiv -2 \equiv 2$.
        *   If $(r_a, r_b) = (2, 0)$: $d' \equiv -2 - 2n$. Pick $n$ even $\implies d' \equiv -2 \equiv 2$.
        *   If $(r_a, r_b) = (0, 2)$: $d' \equiv 2(-1)^n - 2n$. Pick $n$ even $\implies d' \equiv 2(1) - 0 = 2$.
        *   If $(r_a, r_b) = (2, 2)$: $d' \equiv 2(-1)^n - 2 - 2n$. Pick $n$ odd $\implies d' \equiv 2(-1) - 2 - 2 = -6 \equiv 2$.
    *   **Case: $a, b$ are odd and $a \equiv b \pmod 4$.** We require $d' \equiv 0 \pmod 4$.
        $d' \equiv (-1)^n b - a - 2n \pmod 4$.
        Pick $n$ even. Then $d' \equiv (1)b - a - 2(\text{even}) \equiv b - a \pmod 4$.
        Since $b \equiv a \pmod 4$, $d' \equiv 0 \pmod 4$.
    In all cases, we can pick a parity for $n \ge N$ to satisfy the required residue.

**Stage B: Linear Step Calculation**
Starting from $(a', b')$, apply $x \mapsto x+2$ to both numbers for $k$ steps.
The state becomes $(a'', b'') = (a' + 2k, b' + 2k)$.
We want to reach a state where $3a'' = b'' + 2$.
$3(a' + 2k) = (b' + 2k) + 2$
$3a' + 6k = b' + 2k + 2$
$4k = b' - 3a' + 2 = (b' - a') + 2 - 2a'$
Let $d' = b' - a'$. Then $4k = d' + 2 - 2a'$.
1.  $k$ is non-negative: Since $d' \ge 2a' - 2$, $d' + 2 - 2a' \ge 0$.
2.  $k$ is an integer:
    *   If $a, b$ even: $d' \equiv 2 \pmod 4$ and $2a'$ is a multiple of 4 (since $a'$ is even).
        $d' + 2 - 2a' \equiv 2 + 2 - 0 = 4 \equiv 0 \pmod 4$.
    *   If $a, b$ odd: $d' \equiv 0 \pmod 4$ and $a'$ is odd, so $2a' \equiv 2 \pmod 4$.
        $d' + 2 - 2a' \equiv 0 + 2 - 2 = 0 \pmod 4$.
    In both cases, $k$ is an integer.

**Stage C: Final Convergence**
Apply $a \mapsto 3a$ and $b \mapsto b+2$ for the final step.
New value of $a = 3a''$.
New value of $b = b'' + 2$.
Since $3a'' = b'' + 2$, the numbers are now equal.

**Conclusion**
The numbers $a$ and $b$ can be made equal if and only if they have the same parity, and if they are odd, $a \equiv b \pmod 4$.
