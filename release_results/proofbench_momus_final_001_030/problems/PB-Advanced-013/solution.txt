### Stage 1: Ideation

**1. Brainstorm: Promising Avenues**
*   **The Inductive Squeeze (Active Strategy):** Establish a lower bound for partial sums of the form $1/b_k$. Use the sorted property $a_1 \leq a_2 \leq \cdots \leq a_n$ to show that $P_k = \prod_{i=k}^n a_i \geq 1$ for all $k \geq 2$. Perform backward induction from $k=n$ to $k=2$, then incorporate $a_1$ via the constraint $\prod a_k = 1$.
*   **Mixing Variables / Stationary Points:** Treat the sum as a function $G(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ where $x_k = \ln a_k$. Show that $G$ is minimized when all $x_k = 0$ (i.e., $a_k = 1$). Use the $n=2$ case to show that moving any two variables closer to each other while preserving their product and order decreases the sum.

**2. Pre-mortem: Red Herrings (Momus)**
*   **Symmetry Bias:** The weights $2^k$ in $b_k$ break the symmetry of the product $a_1 \dots a_n = 1$. A standard AM-GM approach on the whole sum will likely fail because it ignores the specific alignment of the $2^k$ powers with the sorted order.
*   **Forward Induction:** A forward induction starting from $a_1$ is tempting but problematic. The partial products $a_1 \dots a_k$ are less than 1, leading to bounds that diverge or become negative (as seen in the ideation phase). Backward induction on the "tail" products $P_k = a_k \dots a_n$ is more stable because these products are $\geq 1$.

**3. Selection:**
*   **Active Strategy:** **Backward Induction via Recursive Lower Bound.**
*   **Current Draft:** Empty.

**2. Drafting/Refinement (The Council)**
We define the tail products $P_m = \prod_{k=m}^n a_k$ for $m=2, \dots, n$. Since $a_1 \leq a_2 \leq \cdots \leq a_n$ and $a_1 P_2 = 1$, we must have $a_1 \leq 1$, which implies $P_2 \geq 1$. More generally, for any $m \geq 2$, $P_m \geq 1$. (If $P_m < 1$, then $a_m \dots a_n < 1$, implying $a_m < 1$, and consequently $a_1, \dots, a_{m-1} < 1$, which would make $\prod a_i < 1$).

We propose the following inductive hypothesis $H_m$ for $m \in \{2, \dots, n\}$:
\[ \sum_{k=m}^n \frac{1}{b_k} \geq \frac{1}{2^{m-1}(1+P_m^{2^{m-1}})} - \frac{1}{2^{n+1}} \]

**2. Refinement (The Council)**
*Expanding Base Case $H_n$:*
We must show $\frac{1}{2^n(1+a_n^{2^n})} \geq \frac{1}{2^{n-1}(1+a_n^{2^{n-1}})} - \frac{1}{2^{n+1}}$.
Let $u = a_n^{2^{n-1}}$. Since $a_n = P_n \geq 1$, we have $u \geq 1$.
Multiply by $2^{n+1}$: $\frac{2}{1+u^2} + 1 \geq \frac{4}{1+u} \iff \frac{u^2+3}{1+u^2} \geq \frac{4}{1+u} \iff (u^2+3)(u+1) \geq 4u^2+4 \iff u^3+u^2+3u+3 \geq 4u^2+4 \iff u^3-3u^2+3u-1 \geq 0 \iff (u-1)^3 \geq 0$. This holds for $u \geq 1$.

*Expanding Inductive Step ($H_{m+1} \implies H_m$):*
Assume $H_{m+1}$ holds. Then $\sum_{k=m}^n \frac{1}{b_k} \geq \frac{1}{2^m(1+a_m^{2^m})} + \frac{1}{2^m(1+P_{m+1}^{2^m})} - \frac{1}{2^{n+1}}$.
We need to prove: $\frac{1}{2^m(1+a_m^{2^m})} + \frac{1}{2^m(1+P_{m+1}^{2^m})} \geq \frac{1}{2^{m-1}(1+P_m^{2^{m-1}})}$.
Let $x = P_m^{2^{m-1}}$ and $y = a_m^{2^{m-1}}$. Then $a_m^{2^m} = y^2$ and $P_{m+1}^{2^m} = (P_m/a_m)^{2^m} = (x/y)^2$.
The inequality is $\frac{1}{2^m(1+y^2)} + \frac{1}{2^m(1+x^2/y^2)} \geq \frac{1}{2^{m-1}(1+x)}$.
Multiplying by $2^{m-1}$: $\frac{1}{2(1+y^2)} + \frac{y^2}{2(y^2+x^2)} \geq \frac{1}{1+x}$.
Let $f(y) = \frac{1}{1+y^2} + \frac{y^2}{y^2+x^2}$. Its derivative is $f'(y) = 2y [\frac{x^2}{(y^2+x^2)^2} - \frac{1}{(1+y^2)^2}]$.
Setting $f'(y)=0$ gives $x(1+y^2) = y^2+x^2$, which yields $y^2 = x$ (since $x \geq 1$).
At the minimum $y^2=x$, $f(\sqrt{x}) = \frac{1}{1+x} + \frac{x}{x+x^2} = \frac{2}{1+x}$.
Thus, $f(y) \geq \frac{2}{1+x}$ for all $y > 0$. The step is proved.

### Stage 3: Final Synthesis

**The Architect's Log (Hemingway Style):**
The problem looked like a symmetric inequality, but the weights told a different story. We tried going forward from the first term, but the products grew thin and the bounds failed. We turned around. By looking at the tail of the product, we found a recursive structure. Each step of the induction was a small battle against a cubic polynomial and a derivative. The sorted order of the numbers kept the variables in the safe zone where the inequality holds. At the very end, the product of the whole set pulled everything back to $1/2$. The math was clean. It held.

**The Final Blueprint:**

**Problem:**
For an integer $n \geq 2$, let $a_{1} \leq a_{2} \leq \cdots \leq a_{n}$ be positive real numbers such that $a_{1} a_{2} \cdots a_{n}=1$. Let $b_{k}=2^{k}(1+a_{k}^{2^{k}})$. Prove that:
\[ \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{b_k} \geq \frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{2^{n+1}} \]

**Proof:**
Define the tail products $P_m = \prod_{k=m}^n a_k$ for $m=2, 3, \dots, n$. Since the $a_i$ are positive and $a_1 \leq a_2 \leq \cdots \leq a_n$ with $\prod_{i=1}^n a_i = 1$, the product of the $m-1$ smallest terms satisfies $a_1 a_2 \dots a_{m-1} \leq 1$. Consequently, $P_m = \frac{1}{a_1 \dots a_{m-1}} \geq 1$ for all $m \in \{2, 3, \dots, n\}$.

We prove by induction on $m$, descending from $n$ to $2$, that:
\[ \sum_{k=m}^n \frac{1}{b_k} \geq \frac{1}{2^{m-1}(1+P_m^{2^{m-1}})} - \frac{1}{2^{n+1}} \]

**Base Case ($m=n$):**
We show $\frac{1}{2^n(1+a_n^{2^n})} \geq \frac{1}{2^{n-1}(1+a_n^{2^{n-1}})} - \frac{1}{2^{n+1}}$.
Let $u = a_n^{2^{n-1}}$. Since $a_n = P_n \geq 1$, we have $u \geq 1$.
Multiplying the desired inequality by $2^{n+1}$ gives:
$\frac{2}{1+u^2} + 1 \geq \frac{4}{1+u} \iff \frac{u^2+3}{1+u^2} \geq \frac{4}{1+u}$
$(u^2+3)(u+1) \geq 4(1+u^2) \iff u^3 + u^2 + 3u + 3 \geq 4u^2 + 4 \iff u^3 - 3u^2 + 3u - 1 \geq 0$.
As $u^3 - 3u^2 + 3u - 1 = (u-1)^3$, and $u \geq 1$, the inequality holds.

**Inductive Step:**
Assume the hypothesis holds for $m+1$. We show it for $m$:
$\sum_{k=m}^n \frac{1}{b_k} = \frac{1}{b_m} + \sum_{k=m+1}^n \frac{1}{b_k} \geq \frac{1}{2^m(1+a_m^{2^m})} + \frac{1}{2^m(1+P_{m+1}^{2^m})} - \frac{1}{2^{n+1}}$.
To satisfy the hypothesis for $m$, we require:
$\frac{1}{2^m(1+a_m^{2^m})} + \frac{1}{2^m(1+P_{m+1}^{2^m})} \geq \frac{1}{2^{m-1}(1+P_m^{2^{m-1}})}$.
Let $x = P_m^{2^{m-1}}$ and $y = a_m^{2^{m-1}}$. Since $P_m = a_m P_{m+1}$, we have $P_{m+1}^{2^m} = (x/y)^2$ and $a_m^{2^m} = y^2$. The inequality becomes:
$\frac{1}{2^m(1+y^2)} + \frac{1}{2^m(1+x^2/y^2)} \geq \frac{1}{2^{m-1}(1+x)} \iff \frac{1}{1+y^2} + \frac{y^2}{y^2+x^2} \geq \frac{2}{1+x}$.
Let $f(y) = \frac{1}{1+y^2} + \frac{y^2}{y^2+x^2}$. The derivative is $f'(y) = \frac{-2y}{(1+y^2)^2} + \frac{2yx^2}{(y^2+x^2)^2}$.
Solving $f'(y)=0$ for $y>0$ yields $x^2(1+y^2)^2 = (y^2+x^2)^2$, which simplifies to $x(1+y^2) = y^2+x^2$ (since $x \geq 1$), giving $y^2=x$.
The minimum value is $f(\sqrt{x}) = \frac{1}{1+x} + \frac{x}{x+x^2} = \frac{2}{1+x}$.
Thus, $f(y) \geq \frac{2}{1+x}$ for all $y$, confirming the inductive step.

**Final Summation:**
For $m=2$, we have $\sum_{k=2}^n \frac{1}{b_k} \geq \frac{1}{2(1+P_2^2)} - \frac{1}{2^{n+1}}$.
Adding the first term $\frac{1}{b_1} = \frac{1}{2(1+a_1^2)}$:
$\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{b_k} \geq \frac{1}{2(1+a_1^2)} + \frac{1}{2(1+P_2^2)} - \frac{1}{2^{n+1}}$.
Since $a_1 a_2 \dots a_n = 1$, we have $a_1 = 1/P_2$. Substituting this into the first term:
$\frac{1}{2(1+1/P_2^2)} + \frac{1}{2(1+P_2^2)} = \frac{P_2^2}{2(P_2^2+1)} + \frac{1}{2(P_2^2+1)} = \frac{P_2^2+1}{2(P_2^2+1)} = \frac{1}{2}$.
Therefore, $\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{b_k} \geq \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2^{n+1}}$.
