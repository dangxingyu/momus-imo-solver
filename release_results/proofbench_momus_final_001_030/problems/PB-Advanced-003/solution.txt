### Part 2: The Final Synthesis

**The Architect's Log (Hemingway Style):**
We analyzed the circles and the points of contact. We identified the center of homothety $P$ that scales the incircle to the circumcircle. This point lies on the radical axes formed at each vertex. We then measured the power of the circumcenter. By proving a tangency condition at vertex $A$, we found the circumcenter has equal power to all three circumcircles. With two points of equal power identified, a radical axis is formed. Since $P$ lies inside the circle and $O$ lies outside, the axis must cut the circles twice. These are the points $X$ and $Y$.

**The Final Blueprint (Reviewer-Ready):**

**The Problem:**
Let $ABC$ be an acute non-isosceles triangle. Let $I$ be the incenter and $\omega$ the circumcircle. Let $D, E, F$ be the feet of the internal angle bisectors. Let $\omega_A$ be the circle tangent to $AB, AC$ and internally tangent to $\omega$ at $T_A$. Similarly define $T_B, T_C$. Prove there exist points $X, Y$ common to the circumcircles of $ADT_A, BET_B, CFT_C$.

**Proof:**

**I. The Radical Center $P$ and the Radical Axis**
Let $\gamma$ be the incircle (center $I$, radius $r$) and $\omega$ the circumcircle (center $O$, radius $R$).
1.  **Existence of $P$:** There exists a unique external center of homothety $H$ mapping $\gamma$ to $\omega$. The ratio is $k = R/r$. Since $R > 2r$ (Euler's Inequality), $k > 1$. $H$ is a point $P$ on the line $OI$ such that $\vec{OP} = \frac{R}{R-r}\vec{OI}$.
2.  **Collinearity:** For vertex $A$, let $h_A$ be the homothety centered at $A$ mapping $\gamma \to \omega_A$. Let $h_{T_A}$ be the homothety centered at $T_A$ mapping $\omega_A \to \omega$. The composition $H = h_{T_A} \circ h_A$ maps $\gamma \to \omega$. By the Composition of Homotheties Theorem, the center $P$ lies on the line connecting the centers of $h_A$ and $h_{T_A}$. Thus, $P$ lies on $A T_A$.
3.  **Equal Power:** Let $C_A$ be the circumcircle of $ADT_A$. Since $A$ and $T_A$ are on both $C_A$ and $\omega$, the line $A T_A$ is the radical axis of $(C_A, \omega)$. Thus, $\text{Pow}_{C_A}(P) = \text{Pow}_{\omega}(P)$. Applying this logic to $B$ and $C$, $P$ lies on $B T_B$ and $C T_C$. Therefore:
    $$\text{Pow}_{C_A}(P) = \text{Pow}_{C_B}(P) = \text{Pow}_{C_C}(P) = \text{Pow}_{\omega}(P)$$
    $P$ is the radical center of the three circles.

**II. The Circumcenter $O$ as a Point on the Radical Axis**
We demonstrate that $\text{Pow}_{C_A}(O) = \text{Pow}_{C_B}(O) = \text{Pow}_{C_C}(O) = R^2$.
1.  **Tangency at $A$:** In $\triangle ABC$, the altitude from $A$ and the radius $OA$ are isogonal with respect to the bisector $AD$. Let $\angle B, \angle C$ be the angles at vertices $B, C$. Then $\angle OAD = |\angle OAB - \angle DAB| = |(90^\circ - C) - A/2| = |B-C|/2$.
2.  **Angle in $C_A$:** A known property of the mixtilinear incircle is that the line $T_A D$ passes through the midpoint $M'$ of the major arc $BC$. The angle $\angle AT_A D = \angle AT_A M'$ subtends arc $AM'$ in $\omega$. Since $M'$ is the midpoint of arc $BAC$, the arc $M'C = 180^\circ - A$. Arc $AC = 2B$. Thus arc $AM' = |(180^\circ - A) - 2B| = |B-C|$. The inscribed angle is $\angle AT_A D = |B-C|/2$.
3.  **Result:** Since $\angle OAD = \angle AT_A D$, by the Converse of the Alternate Segment Theorem, the line $OA$ is tangent to $C_A$ at $A$. Thus, $\text{Pow}_{C_A}(O) = OA^2 = R^2$. By symmetry of the geometric construction for $B$ and $C$, $\text{Pow}_{C_B}(O) = R^2$ and $\text{Pow}_{C_C}(O) = R^2$.

**III. Coaxality and Intersection**
1.  **Radical Axis:** Points $P$ and $O$ have equal power with respect to $C_A, C_B,$ and $C_C$. In a non-isosceles triangle, $P \neq O$. Thus, $C_A, C_B, C_C$ are coaxal with radical axis $OP$.
2.  **Existence of $X, Y$:** Let $d = OI$. The distance $OP = \frac{R}{R-r}d$. The power of $P$ with respect to $\omega$ is:
    $$\text{Pow}_{\omega}(P) = OP^2 - R^2 = \frac{R^2 d^2}{(R-r)^2} - R^2 = R^2 \left( \frac{d^2 - (R-r)^2}{(R-r)^2} \right)$$
    Using Euler's Theorem ($d^2 = R^2 - 2Rr$):
    $$\text{Pow}_{\omega}(P) = R^2 \left( \frac{R^2 - 2Rr - (R^2 - 2Rr + r^2)}{(R-r)^2} \right) = \frac{-R^2 r^2}{(R-r)^2}$$
    Since $\text{Pow}_{C_A}(P) < 0$ and $\text{Pow}_{C_A}(O) = R^2 > 0$, the radical axis $OP$ must intersect the circles at two distinct points $X$ and $Y$. Since the circles are coaxal, they all pass through $X$ and $Y$.
