**Part 2: The Final Synthesis**

**The Architect's Log (Hemingway Style):**
We built the proof from the feet of the perpendiculars. We showed that if the angle at $A$ stays constant, the feet stay on a circle. We proved that this circle is the auxiliary circle of a conic. We derived the reflection property from the distance sum of the ellipse. We showed that the two foci must mirror each other across the angle's bisector. We proved that the second focus sees a constant angle because the reflections of the first focus are locked on a circle centered at the second. The logic is self-contained.

**The Final Blueprint:**

**The Problem**
Let $\angle XYZ = \alpha$ be an acute angle with $\alpha \ne 60^\circ$, and let $A$ be a point inside $\angle XYZ$. Prove that there exists $D\ne A$ inside $\angle XYZ$ and $\theta\in (0,2\pi )$ such that for points $B \in \overrightarrow{YX}$ and $C \in \overrightarrow{YZ}$:
\[ \angle BAC = \alpha \quad \implies \quad \angle BDC = \theta \]

**Proof**

**1. The Pedal Circle of Point A**
Let $P, Q,$ and $R$ be the projections of point $A$ onto the lines $YX, YZ,$ and $BC$ respectively.
In quadrilateral $YPAQ$, $\angle APY = \angle AQY = 90^\circ$. Thus $YPAQ$ is a cyclic quadrilateral with diameter $AY$. The side $PQ$ is a chord subtending the angle $\alpha$ at the circumference, so $PQ = AY \sin \alpha$.
Since $\angle APB = \angle ARB = 90^\circ$, points $A, P, B, R$ lie on a circle with diameter $AB$. The directed angle of lines $(RP, RA) = (BP, BA) = (YX, AB)$.
Since $\angle AQC = \angle ARC = 90^\circ$, points $A, Q, C, R$ lie on a circle with diameter $AC$. The directed angle $(RA, RQ) = (CA, CQ) = (AC, YZ)$.
Summing these, the angle $(RP, RQ) = (YX, AB) + (AC, YZ) = (YX, YZ) + (AC, AB) = \alpha + \angle BAC$.
Given $\angle BAC = \alpha$, we have $(RP, RQ) = 2\alpha$.
Since $P$ and $Q$ are fixed and the angle $\angle PRQ$ is constant, $R$ must lie on a fixed circle $\mathcal{C}$ passing through $P$ and $Q$. The radius $a$ of $\mathcal{C}$ is $a = \frac{PQ}{2 \sin(2\alpha)} = \frac{AY \sin \alpha}{4 \sin \alpha \cos \alpha} = \frac{AY}{4 \cos \alpha}$.

**2. The Conic and the Focal Reflection Property**
Let $\mathcal{K}$ be the conic with focus $A$ whose auxiliary circle (the locus of the feet of perpendiculars from a focus to the tangents) is $\mathcal{C}$. Since $P, Q,$ and $R$ lie on $\mathcal{C}$, the lines $YX, YZ,$ and $BC$ are tangents to $\mathcal{K}$.
Let $D$ be the second focus of $\mathcal{K}$. The center $O$ of circle $\mathcal{C}$ is the midpoint of $AD$. 
Let $L$ be a tangent to $\mathcal{K}$ at $T$, and let $S$ be the reflection of $A$ across $L$. Since the projection $R$ of $A$ onto $L$ is the midpoint of $AS$ and $O$ is the midpoint of $AD$, by the Midline Theorem in $\triangle ASD$, $OR = \frac{1}{2} DS$. 
Since $R$ lies on the circle $\mathcal{C}$ with radius $a$, $OR = a$. Thus $DS = 2a$. 
This proves that for any tangent to $\mathcal{K}$, the reflection of focus $A$ across that tangent lies on a circle $\Omega$ centered at focus $D$ with radius $2a$.

**3. Constancy of the Angle at D**
Let $S_1, S_2,$ and $S_3$ be the reflections of $A$ across lines $YX, YZ,$ and $BC$ respectively. By Section 2, $S_1, S_2, S_3$ lie on the circle $\Omega$ centered at $D$.
$B$ is the intersection of $YX$ and $BC$. Since $YX$ is the perpendicular bisector of $AS_1$ and $BC$ is the perpendicular bisector of $AS_3$, $B$ is the circumcenter of $\triangle AS_1S_3$. Thus $BS_1 = BS_3$.
In $\triangle BDS_1$ and $\triangle BDS_3$:
1. $DS_1 = DS_3$ (radii of $\Omega$)
2. $BS_1 = BS_3$ (circumcenter $B$)
3. $BD$ is common.
By SSS congruence, $\triangle BDS_1 \cong \triangle BDS_3$, so $\angle S_1DB = \angle S_3DB = \frac{1}{2} \angle S_1DS_3$.
Similarly, $C$ is the circumcenter of $\triangle AS_3S_2$, so $CS_3 = CS_2$. By SSS, $\triangle CDS_3 \cong \triangle CDS_2$, so $\angle S_3DC = \angle S_2DC = \frac{1}{2} \angle S_3DS_2$.
Then $\angle BDC = \angle BDS_3 + \angle S_3DC = \frac{1}{2} (\angle S_1DS_3 + \angle S_3DS_2) = \frac{1}{2} \angle S_1DS_2$.
Since $D, S_1,$ and $S_2$ are fixed by $A$ and the rays $YX, YZ$, the angle $\angle BDC$ is a constant $\theta$.

**4. Existence and Distinctness of D**
$Y$ lies on the perpendicular bisectors of $AS_1$ and $AS_2$, so $YA = YS_1 = YS_2$. Thus $Y$ is the circumcenter of $\triangle AS_1S_2$. 
In $\triangle YDS_1$ and $\triangle YDS_2$: $YS_1 = YS_2$, $DS_1 = DS_2$, and $YD$ is common. By SSS, $\triangle YDS_1 \cong \triangle YDS_2$, so $\angle S_1YD = \angle S_2YD$. 
Because $YX$ bisects $\angle AYS_1$ and $YZ$ bisects $\angle AYS_2$, the rays $YA$ and $YD$ are isogonal conjugates with respect to $\angle XYZ$. Thus $D$ lies inside $\angle XYZ$.
- **Parabola Case:** A parabola is a conic where the second focus $D$ is at infinity. This occurs if the center $O$ of the auxiliary circle is at infinity, requiring the radius $a \to \infty$. From Section 1, $a = \frac{AY}{4 \cos \alpha}$. For $a \to \infty$, $\cos \alpha = 0 \implies \alpha = 90^\circ$. Since $\alpha$ is acute, $D$ is a finite point.
- **$D = A$ Case:** If $D = A$, $\mathcal{K}$ is a circle. For a circle tangent to $YX, YZ, BC$, $A$ is the incenter or an excenter. 
If $A$ is the incenter, $\angle BAC = 90^\circ + \alpha/2 = \alpha \implies \alpha = 180^\circ$ (impossible). 
If $A$ is the excenter opposite $Y$, $\angle BAC = 90^\circ - \alpha/2 = \alpha \implies \alpha = 60^\circ$.
Since $\alpha \neq 60^\circ$, $D \neq A$. Q.E.D.
